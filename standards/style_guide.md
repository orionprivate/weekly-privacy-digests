Style Guide
Orion Private State Privacy Digest

This file governs voice, wording, and formatting. What to write and where it goes is governed by editorial_standard.md. How evidence is cited is governed by source_hierarchy.md.

Sentences

Short declarative sentences. One idea per sentence. Past tense for what happened, present tense for what is in force.

No em dashes. No semicolons. Colons sparingly. Periods do the work.

No rhetorical triads. Two examples name a pattern. Three decorate it.

No throat-clearing openers. Notably, importantly, it is worth noting, and their relatives are deleted on sight. If a fact matters, its placement says so.

Diction

Plain words over technical or legal jargon, except where a term of art is the accurate word. Then use the term of art and do not paraphrase it.

Adjectives of magnitude require a stated basis in the same entry. A landmark is landmark by a number, or it is not called one. Sweeping, major, historic, and unprecedented follow the same rule.

No marketing language. The digest informs. It does not sell.

No authorship signposts. Our read, we think, in our view, and every variant are banned. The publication owns every sentence, and the entry structure marks where fact ends and analysis begins.

The digest never refers to itself, its sections, its standards, or its process in the body of an issue.

Formatting

GitHub-flavored markdown, no HTML. Section names in capitals as given in editorial_standard.md. Entry headers on their own line: number, item name, action word.

The four entry moves appear as labeled lines in fixed order: What changed. Why it matters. Who this touches. Next step.

Links render as linked source names, never raw URLs. Link formatting rules live in source_hierarchy.md.

No emoji. No decorative bullets. No horizontal rules between entries. White space and the fixed structure carry the hierarchy.

Numbers and dates

Dates are written in full on first mention: August 1, 2026. Money is written in numerals with the unit: 1.35 million dollars. Bill identifiers use the legislature's own format: S.B. 1295. Statutes use official short names on every mention.
