## COMPLIANCE HORIZON

01. California Delete Act deletion processing. Prepare.
What changed. Registered data brokers must begin processing consumer deletion requests submitted through California's DROP platform beginning August 1, 2026.
Why it matters. The DROP portal launched January 1, 2026, allowing California residents to file a single deletion request to hundreds of data brokers, and data brokers must process delete requests submitted through DROP beginning August 1, 2026, with defined compliance timelines. The launch established the intake mechanism. The August date converts it into an operational obligation, and brokers without an automated deletion pipeline face per-request exposure once requests flow.
Who this touches. Privacy Office, Legal and Compliance, Data and Analytics
Next step. Prepare: data broker compliance leads confirm DROP intake and deletion workflows before August 1, 2026.
Source: California Delete Act (SB 362) and DROP platform.

## MOVING

02. FTC and DOJ settlement with RentGrow. Act now.
What changed. On July 9, 2026, the FTC announced that RentGrow, a tenant screening consumer reporting agency, will pay 2.25 million dollars to settle allegations it violated the Fair Credit Reporting Act and the FTC Act, with a complaint and proposed stipulated order filed by the DOJ.
Why it matters. RentGrow will pay 2.25 million dollars to settle allegations that it violated the Fair Credit Reporting Act, including by failing to use reasonable procedures to ensure the accuracy of its reports, and the FTC Act. The company failed to prevent duplicate case records and multiple entries for the same criminal or eviction action, which gave the false impression that applicants had more convictions or eviction suits than they actually had, and the FTC alleged the company was aware but failed to act until the investigation began. The settlement also alleged RentGrow told consumers who successfully disputed information that it had notified the property manager, while telling the property owner there was no change. The read: this is the accuracy-and-dispute side of consumer reporting, not notice language, and any firm compiling or reselling background data now has a concrete template for what reasonable procedures and dispute follow-through must look like. Waiting to audit report-assembly and dispute logic invites the same four counts.
Who this touches. Legal and Compliance, Privacy Office, Product and Engineering
Next step. Act now: compliance counsel audits report accuracy procedures and post-dispute notification practices against the RentGrow order.
Source: United States v. RentGrow, Inc. (complaint and proposed stipulated order), FTC press release.

03. Massachusetts youth social media bill. Awareness only.
What changed. On July 9, 2026, the Massachusetts Senate passed S.3164 by a vote of 38-2, a bill requiring social media platforms to default minors into settings that disable addictive features.
Why it matters. Senators passed the bill by a vote of 38-2, after adding amendments that address overnight push notifications, age verification techniques and whether certain open source sites fall under the definition of social media. Under the bill, autoplay, infinite scroll, algorithmic feeds that recommend content from accounts users do not follow, and other endless scrolling functions would be turned off by default for minors. The bill also requires apps to default off the visibility and sharing of a minor's precise geolocation data and to limit minors' messaging and content sharing to connected accounts, with the geolocation default changeable only by the minor with parental consent. The read: this passed one chamber and diverges from the House approach, so it is not law and not yet a build requirement. It matters now because it signals where a large-market state is heading on default-off design and age assurance, and product teams gain lead time by mapping the default architecture early.
Who this touches. Legal and Compliance, Product and Engineering, Government Affairs
Next step. Awareness only: government affairs tracks S.3164 through conference with the House bill.
Source: S.3164, Massachusetts Senate.
